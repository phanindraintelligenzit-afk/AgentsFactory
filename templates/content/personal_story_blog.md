I need to tell you about the worst day of my career. And why I'm grateful it happened.

## The Parking Lot

Three years ago, I sat in a client's parking lot and cried.

Not quietly. Not gracefully. Full-on staring at the steering wheel, wondering if I'd built my entire business around something that didn't actually work.

We'd been working on {topic} automation for a {{industry}} client for three months. Every week brought a new edge case, a new coordination failure, a new moment of "wait, that's not how I thought this works." The client was losing patience. I was losing confidence. And the technology was — let's say "underperforming."

On that particular Thursday, we'd just rolled back a deployment for the third time. The client's operations manager looked at me with the kind of polite skepticism that's worse than anger.

I excused myself. Got in my car. And sat there.

## The Phone Call

That evening, I called someone I trust. I said the thing I'd been thinking for weeks:

"I think this {topic} automation thing might actually be impossible at this level of complexity."

There was a long pause.

Then: *"What if it's just impossible THAT way?"*

It wasn't profound. It wasn't an insight that arrived with thunder and lightning. It was a simple reframe. But it cracked everything open.

## The Rebuild

The next week, we stopped building. We stopped configuring. We stopped trying to make the tools work.

Instead, we got a whiteboard and spent five days doing nothing but mapping the client's actual {topic} process. Not the documented version. Not the idealized version. The real version — including all the workarounds, the "I'll just check this real quick" moments, and the steps that existed for reasons nobody could remember.

What we found was humbling:

- **40% of the process steps were unnecessary.** They existed because of historical accidents, not operational requirements.
- **Three approval steps** existed because of a single incident in 2019 that everyone had forgotten about.
- **Two data re-entry points** existed because two departments didn't trust each other's data quality.

We didn't need better automation. We needed a better process.

## The Result

Once we stripped out the noise, the {topic} automation practically built itself. Two weeks of clean development. One week of testing. Deployed on a Monday morning with zero issues.

The results:

- **15 hours/week → 2 hours/week** on {topic} administration
- **Error rate: 12% → 0.8%**
- **Client satisfaction: 7/10 → 9.2/10**
- **Three referrals** from that single client

That project — the one I almost quit — became our best case study. The workflow we designed became our template for an entire industry vertical.

## What I Learned

### 1. Failure Is a Process Problem in Disguise

When {topic} automation isn't working, the instinct is to blame the tools. Upgrade the platform. Try a different vendor. Add more features.

Rarely is the technology the problem. Almost always, the process being automated is more complex than it needs to be. Automate a mess, get a faster mess.

### 2. Simplicity Beats Sophistication

The most elegant automation I've ever built was also the simplest. It did three things, did them reliably, and never needed maintenance.

The most sophisticated system I ever built was a nightmare. It handled every edge case, anticipated every scenario, and broke if someone looked at it wrong.

Simple and reliable beats complex and fragile. Every time.

### 3. The Best Breakthroughs Come After the Worst Days

I've noticed a pattern in my career: the projects that almost broke me became the most valuable. Not because suffering is virtuous — it's not. But because the projects that push you to the edge force you to question assumptions you didn't know you had.

That parking lot day forced me to question whether I was automating processes or improving them. The answer changed everything.

## For Anyone in Their Own Parking Lot Moment

If you're in the middle of a {topic} automation project that feels impossible right now, I want you to know two things:

**First:** The feeling is normal. Every automation practitioner has a parking lot story. The ones who say they don't are either new or not being honest.

**Second:** The mess is not the message. The mess is just information. It's telling you something about the process that you need to hear before the automation can work.

Step back. Map the process. Remove the noise. Then automate.

The other side is worth it.
